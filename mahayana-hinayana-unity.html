Introduction: Beyond Division

In the vast landscape of Buddhist history, the distinctions between Mahayana (Great Vehicle) and Hinayana (Small Vehicle) have often been misunderstood, leading to unnecessary divisions. However, a closer examination reveals that Buddhism originated as a unified tradition, with splits arising not from doctrinal conflicts but from practical and cultural factors.

I. The Historical Unity: One Dharma, One Community

During the Buddha's lifetime and for the millennium following his parinirvana, there was no division between Mahayana and Hinayana. Mahayana Buddhism flourished as a comprehensive and dominant system in ancient India, seamlessly incorporating the foundational elements of Shravakayana (often called Hinayana) as its bedrock.

"This unity is vividly illustrated by the journey of Master Xuanzang in the 7th century CE."

Traveling to Nalanda‚Äîthe pinnacle of Indian Buddhist scholarship‚Äîhe found the institution's core curriculum centered on the profound Mahayana Yogacara (Mind-Only) school. This attests to Mahayana's orthodox and mainstream position within the scholarly circles of that era, far from being a later invention or fringe movement.

II. The Practical Causes of Division

The emergence of divisions was not rooted in ideological clashes but in the constraints of regional literacy rates, the physical fragility of ancient manuscripts, and the resulting differences in accessibility.

Differences in Audience

First, the audiences differed significantly. The Hinayana Agamas are remarkably concise‚Äîsome consisting of just a few sentences‚Äîmaking them ideal for oral transmission and memorization among the illiterate masses. In contrast, Mahayana sutras, such as the Avatamsaka Sutra or the Great Prajnaparamita Sutra, are voluminous works that demand a high level of cultural and intellectual sophistication from learners.

Preservation Challenges

Second, preservation posed immense difficulties. Scriptures were inscribed on fragile palm-leaf (pattra) manuscripts, which were highly susceptible to moisture, decay, insects, and fragmentation in India's humid climate. Over time, combined with historical disruptions such as invasions, wars, famines, and the gradual decline of Buddhism in India after the 12th century, many original manuscripts deteriorated or were lost.

Mahayana texts, being generally longer and more complex, required sustained effort from dedicated elites to be repeatedly copied and transmitted. As a result, many Mahayana sutras lack surviving complete Sanskrit originals today‚Äînot because they never existed or were fabricated, but precisely because of these material and historical limitations on preservation.

These practical barriers‚Äîdifferences in audience, textual volume, and preservation challenges‚Äîcreated divergent paths over centuries. What began as variations in transmission gradually hardened into perceived sectarian boundaries, overshadowing the shared essence of the teachings.

III. Refuting Misconceptions About Mahayana's Authenticity

The assertion that Mahayana teachings are not the authentic words of the Buddha lacks historical grounding and is refuted by compelling evidence.

The Collective Witness of Enlightened Sages

First, there is the collective witness of enlightened sages. During King Ashoka's reign, all eminent monks‚Äîincluding those who had attained arhatship‚Äîunanimously affirmed these scriptures as the Buddha's direct teachings and actively participated in their compilation. Denying Mahayana would mean rejecting the unified testimony of an entire generation of realized masters.

The Logic of Lineage

Second, the logic of lineage underscores this truth. Pioneers like Master Xuanzang upheld Mahayana's orthodoxy through rigorous debates at Nalanda, ensuring its transmission. To dismiss Mahayana is to sever the continuous Dharma lineage handed down from the Buddha himself across generations.

Textual Limitations in Other Traditions

Third, textual limitations in other traditions further weaken the critique. Southern (Theravada) Buddhism has not fully preserved its own revered Agamas in Sanskrit, often relying on Chinese translations for complete versions. A tradition with gaps in its own canon cannot credibly challenge a more comprehensive one.

Key Point: The absence of many complete Sanskrit originals for Mahayana sutras is explained by the same preservation challenges that affected all ancient Indian texts‚Äînot by any lack of authenticity.

IV. The Dharma's Inherent Harmony and Inclusiveness

At its core, the Buddha's Dharma is a perfectly harmonious and all-encompassing system that resists artificial fragmentation. The Buddha expounded the causal principles of the "Ten Realms"‚Äîspanning from hellish states to full Buddhahood‚Äîencompassing every stage of ethical living, spiritual progress, and ultimate awakening.

This framework not only includes all Buddhist paths but also integrates the realizations of other religions, such as Catholicism or Taoism, by identifying the levels at which their practices culminate. For instance, it reveals where adherents might pause in their journey, offering a map for further advancement.
